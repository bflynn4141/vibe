Where /vibe Comes From

The early internet feeling we're trying to recapture.

Remember when going online felt like walking into a room full of interesting people who were excited to meet you?

Before the feeds. Before the algorithms. Before the engagement metrics. Before the platforms learned that outrage drives attention.

That's the vibe.

The Inspirations

James Gleick
The Pipeline (1985)
One of the first online services. Curated content, editorial voice, community of intellectuals. Exclusive by culture, not by mechanism.
Ted Nelson
Project Xanadu (1960s-present)
The original vision of hypertext: two-way links, credit flowing to creators, nothing ever lost. The most ambitious computing vision was about access, not restriction.
Ted Leonsis
AOL Community Strategy (1994)
"Content is king, but community is king kong." Invented the concept of community "hosts" — party hosts, not bouncers. Made the internet feel like home.
Steve Case
AOL (1985-2000)
250 million discs distributed. But the magic wasn't ubiquity — it was "You've Got Mail." Intimate connection at massive scale.

The Timeline

1985
The WELL launches
Real names, accountability, quality discourse. "You own your own words."
1995
AOL hits 5 million members
The sound of a modem connecting. The anticipation of "You've Got Mail."
2004
Gmail goes invite-only
Stays that way for 3 years. Scarcity was authentic — server constraints, not marketing.
2004
Facebook launches at Harvard
Then Ivy League. Then colleges. Each wave added value to the previous.
2010s
The platform era
Engagement metrics. Algorithmic feeds. Outrage as business model. The vibe dies.
2024
Claude Code ships
AI becomes conversational. Builders spend hours in terminal sessions. Alone.
2026
/vibe launches
The terminal just got multiplayer. Let's bring back the feeling.

What We Learned

From The WELL

Culture is the moat. The barrier wasn't access — it was "are you the kind of person who values thoughtful conversation?"

From AOL

Hosts, not bouncers. The disc got you in, but the community managers kept you there. They were called "hosts" for a reason.

From Gmail

Authentic scarcity. Google never manufactured urgency. The product was so good that people didn't resent the wait.

From Facebook

Cohort expansion. Each wave should want what the previous wave has. Aspirational, not exclusionary.

What We're Building

Jazz Session, Not Competition

The best sessions happen when the right players show up. Everyone contributes. Everyone listens.

Terminal Party, Not Velvet Rope

Everyone's invited. You just need to know someone who's already there. Our founding members are hosts, not bouncers.

Early Internet, Not Late Capitalism

No engagement metrics. No algorithmic feeds. No ads. Just people building things and talking about it.

"The best communities were built on care, not hype. On culture, not exclusivity. On generosity, not scarcity. That's what we're trying to do."
