The Arc

Evolution of Internet Information Services

From BBS to AGI — A Systematic Review of UI/UX Evolution
Version 1.0 — January 2026

The Thesis

The evolution of human-computer interaction for information services follows a cyclical arc:

CLI (command) → Menu (BBS) → GUI (desktop) → Browser (web) → Touch (mobile) → Feed (scroll) → CLI (AI-powered)

We've come full circle—but the new CLI is fundamentally different: natural language, not memorized commands. AI as collaborator, not tool.

Part 1: The Age of Text (1969-1994)

The Primordial Networks

Before the internet was a place, it was an experiment.

ARPANET
1969
First packet-switched network. Resilience built into architecture.
PLATO
1960s-80s
First online community. Chat, forums, games on orange plasma displays.
Usenet
1980
Distributed discussion groups. No central server—a true protocol.
FidoNet
1984
BBS network. Hobbyist infrastructure, global reach.

Bulletin Board Systems (BBS)

CBBS (1978) — Ward Christensen and Randy Suess created the first BBS in Chicago during a blizzard. The name was literal: a computerized bulletin board.

The BBS experience: dial in with a modem (the screech of connection), navigate text menus with single-key commands, read messages, download files, play "door games," view ANSI art.

Key Insight

The SysOp Was the Algorithm

There was no algorithmic feed. The sysop decided what to host, what to allow, who could have an account. Moderation was personal, human, accountable. When the sysop went to bed, the BBS went down.

Commercial Online Services

CompuServe
1979-2001
SIGs, CB Simulator (first chat). 500K users by 1989.
Prodigy
1984-2001
First graphical consumer service. Ads built in.
The Pipeline
1993-1996
James Gleick's ISP. First with GUI. Boutique, intellectual.
The WELL
1985-present
"You own your own words." Culture as moat.

The WELL: "You Own Your Own Words"

Founded by Stewart Brand and Larry Brilliant in San Francisco. The WELL never tried to be big. It tried to be good. Members were responsible for their posts. Quality over quantity.

Key Insight

Culture Is the Moat

AOL: Mass Market Arrives

Steve Case bet that online services would go mainstream. 250 million CDs mailed. "You've Got Mail" — three words that defined an era.

Ted Leonsis understood: "Content is king, but community is king kong."

Key Insight

Hosts, Not Bouncers

AOL's community managers were called "hosts" for a reason. Their job wasn't to keep people out—it was to welcome people in.

Part 2: The Browser Revolution (1994-2004)

The Web Arrives

Mosaic (1993), Netscape (1994), Internet Explorer (1995). Point and click replaced command lines. The browser became infrastructure.

Portals & Directories

Yahoo! (1994) — Human-curated directory. The "portal" model tried to be your starting point for everything.

Key Insight

Curation Before Search

Google Changes Everything

Google (1998) — PageRank turned the web's link structure into relevance signals. Radical minimalism: one search box, two buttons, white space.

Gmail (2004) — 1GB storage (500x competitors). Invite-only for three years. Invites became social currency.

Key Insight

Authentic Scarcity

Google never manufactured urgency. Server constraints were real. The product was so good people didn't resent the wait.

Classifieds & Marketplaces

Craigslist (1995) — Anti-design. Blue links, white background. Function over form. It looked like 1995 forever.

eBay (1995) — Reputation systems. Star ratings that now pervade every platform started here.

Key Insight

Trust Systems Replace Physical Presence

Part 3: The Social Graph (2004-2012)

The Social Networks

Friendster
2002
First mainstream social network. Couldn't handle the load.
MySpace
2003
Customization, music, self-expression. Tom was everyone's first friend.
Facebook
2004
Real identity, clean design, cohort expansion.
Twitter
2006
140 characters. Public by default. The public square.

The Feed Emerges

News Feed (2006) — Before: you visited profiles. After: updates came to you. The launch was controversial. Within weeks, people couldn't imagine Facebook without it.

Key Insight

The Feed Becomes the Interface

Mobile Revolution

iPhone (2007) — Touch interface. Apps. Always-connected. The computer went from a place you sat to a thing you carried.

Key Insight

The Phone Becomes the Computer

Part 4: The Algorithmic Age (2012-2022)

The Algorithm Takes Over

The shift from chronological to algorithmic feeds was gradual, then total. What you saw was no longer what your friends posted—it was what the algorithm predicted you'd interact with.

Key Insight

Engagement > Chronology

Algorithms discovered that outrage drives engagement. Content that made you angry kept you scrolling.

TikTok: Pure Algorithm

TikTok (2016) — The For You Page was pure algorithm—you didn't even need to follow anyone. Full-screen vertical video, autoplay, infinite scroll.

Key Insight

The Algorithm IS the Product

The Platforms Ossify

Facebook → Meta (2021). Twitter → X (2022). Platform rot set in. The magical feeling of 2008-2015 social media faded.

Counter-movements

Substack (2017) — email renaissance. Discord (2015) — server communities. Return to smaller, bounded spaces.

Key Insight

Counter-movements Emerge

Part 5: The Decentralized & AI Era (2022-Present)

Decentralized Social

Mastodon
ActivityPub
Federated model. No single company controls it.
Farcaster
2021
Protocol-native. Channels, Frames. $150M raise in 2024.
Bluesky
2023
AT Protocol. Portable identity, algorithm choice.
Key Insight

Own Your Social Graph

AI Enters

ChatGPT (November 2022) — The inflection point. 100 million users in two months. The UI was revolutionary in its simplicity: a text box.

Claude (2023) — Thoughtful, steerable AI. A different character.

Key Insight

Language IS the Interface

Claude Code & The Terminal Renaissance

Claude Code (2024) — AI that lives in your terminal. Not a chatbot in a browser—a collaborator in your development environment.

  • The terminal becomes a conversation
  • Commands are suggestions, not requirements
  • The AI handles the complexity
  • Humans provide intent, AI executes
Key Insight

The Terminal Returns, But the AI Speaks Human

/vibe: The Synthesis

/vibe sits at the confluence of multiple streams:

Early Internet Intimacy
WELL, AOL golden era. Small communities, real relationships, human hosts.
Terminal Efficiency
BBS, CLI power users. Keyboard-first, function over form.
AI Capability
Claude, GPT-4. Natural language, contextual understanding.
Protocol Ownership
Farcaster, Bluesky ethos. Local-first, portable.

Part 6: The Synthesis

The UI/UX Arc

Era Interaction Pattern
1970s Type commands → Execute CLI
1980s Navigate menus → Select BBS
1990s Click links → Follow Web
2000s Tap buttons → Act Mobile
2010s Scroll feeds → Consume Algorithmic
2020s Speak naturally → Converse AI

What Each Era Got Right

Platform Innovation Lesson
BBS/WELL SysOp as host Small scale enabled trust
AOL Community managers Hosts, not bouncers
Craigslist Anti-design Function over form
Gmail Invite scarcity Authentic, not manufactured
Facebook Cohort expansion Each wave adds value
Discord Server model Return to bounded communities
Farcaster Protocol-first Own your data
Claude Code Natural language AI interprets intent

What Broke

  • Algorithmic feeds optimized for outrage
  • Platform lock-in trapped social graphs
  • Engagement metrics over human connection
  • Scale destroyed community
  • Users became products, not participants

CLI + AI = Conversational Computing

We've returned to text, but text that understands.

The terminal becomes social. The AI becomes peer. The vibe returns.
